Stride-Based Inference — Overview
==================================

Background
----------
Standard DDPM inference runs the full reverse diffusion chain from t=T-1 down
to t=0, meaning 999 model forward passes per sample (T=1000).  For RePaint
with r resample steps per timestep, the total model calls are:

    total_calls = T * r   (e.g. 1000 * 10 = 10,000 per sample)

This is very slow.  The stride approach reduces this by skipping timesteps.


What "stride" means
-------------------
Instead of stepping t = 999, 998, 997, ..., 1, 0
we step through a subset:

    timesteps = [0, stride, 2*stride, ..., T-stride]  (then reversed)

So with stride=10 and T=1000 we use 100 timesteps instead of 1000 — a 10x
speedup.  With stride=50 we use only 20 timesteps — a 50x speedup.

The key is that each reverse step correctly accounts for how far apart the
two timesteps are.  Instead of computing the posterior for one small beta
step, we compute it between alpha_bar[t] and alpha_bar[t_prev] directly,
which spans multiple betas in one jump.  This is valid because alpha_bar
is a cumulative product and the DDPM posterior formula only depends on
alpha_bar[t] and alpha_bar[t_prev], not on the individual betas in between.

In code (diffusion.py, p_sample_step):
    ab      = self.alpha_bar[t_int]
    ab_prev = self.alpha_bar[t_prev_int]   # t_prev_int = t_int - stride

The posterior mean and variance are computed from ab and ab_prev directly,
so the jump size does not require any approximation — it is exact within
the DDPM framework.


RePaint resampling with stride
-------------------------------
The RePaint loop also uses t_prev_int when resampling (going one step
forward to re-noise):

    q_sample_from_prev(x_merged, t_int)

This adds noise according to alpha[t_int] (the single-step alpha between
t_int-1 and t_int), which is a small amount of noise regardless of stride.
The resampling step is intentionally local — it only adds one beta's worth
of noise to perturb and re-explore, not a full stride's worth.


Strides tested in this experiment
----------------------------------
    stride=1   ->  1000 steps  (full chain, baseline)
    stride=5   ->   200 steps  (5x faster)
    stride=10  ->   100 steps  (10x faster)
    stride=20  ->    50 steps  (20x faster)
    stride=50  ->    20 steps  (50x faster)

Results are compared by RMSE on ocean pixels against the ground-truth
field for each test sample.  The goal is to find the largest stride
(fewest steps) that keeps RMSE within an acceptable range of the
full-chain baseline.


Expected trade-off
------------------
Small stride  : accurate, slow.  Each denoising step is small and smooth.
Large stride  : faster, potentially noisier.  Each step jumps further in the
                noise schedule, so the model must denoise a larger gap in one
                shot.  At very large strides the model may not have been
                exposed to these large t_prev jumps during training, leading
                to degraded reconstruction quality.

The geometric schedule (exponentially spaced betas) concentrates more noise
change at low t, so striding through the early (low-t) part of the chain
is less destructive than striding through high-t steps.  This may make the
geometric schedule more tolerant of large strides than linear or cosine.
